From Policy to Projects

Expert perspectives on the barriers and costs of European CCS deployment

Sebastian Hanss-Mirodone — Master’s Thesis, March 2026
TUM Campus Straubing, Technical University of Munich

The dominant barrier is a lack of durable policy commitment

Across 56 interviews, lack of long-term policy certainty scored nearly double the second-ranked barrier. CCS projects need 15–20-year lifespans to recover costs, yet policy certainty rarely extends beyond a single legislative period.

High upfront CAPEX and missing CO₂ transport & storage infrastructure rank second and third — forming a mutually reinforcing cycle: without infrastructure, costs stay high; without viable business cases, infrastructure investment stalls.

Cement is the most promising near-term application

Roughly 60% of clinker emissions are inherent to the calcination reaction — no fuel switch can eliminate them. Combined with high flue-gas CO₂ concentrations and limited import competition, cement is the deployment priority.

Waste-to-energy attracted broad support because approximately half of its emissions are biogenic, enabling both fossil decarbonization and carbon removal from a single facility.

The structural gap for BECCS is not a cost problem but a market creation problem: biogenic CO₂ capture is not currently recognized under the EU ETS, leaving no compliance revenue stream.

Both panels show 15–32% learning curves from FOAK to NOAK, but experts consistently attributed these reductions to transport and storage infrastructure maturation rather than capture technology improvements.

The inland cost penalty shrinks from €44–46/tCO₂ at FOAK to €5–13/tCO₂ at NOAK, as pipeline networks eliminate rail-based CO₂ transport. Shared infrastructure development is the single most effective cross-sectoral cost lever.

The EU ETS and CCfDs are the most important policy mechanisms

The ordering inverted by sector: cement experts ranked CCfDs first, reflecting the need for revenue certainty over long capital cycles, while BECCS experts ranked the ETS first but elevated reverse auctions alongside CCfDs.

The forthcoming 2026 EU ETS revision — particularly whether negative emissions will be integrated into carbon markets — was identified as the single most consequential near-term policy decision for BECCS.

Europe has built an extensive CCS policy architecture. The instruments required to unlock deployment exist and are operational. What is missing is not the toolbox but the commitment to use it at a scale and duration commensurate with the investment decisions these instruments are designed to enable.

Cement CCS is ready for near-term deployment under existing carbon pricing. BECCS requires dedicated CDR procurement mechanisms. Both pathways are actionable. The barrier is political will, not technology.
